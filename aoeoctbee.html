Bumblebee (High Octane; Deluxe) [AoE]

Allegiance : Autobot
Size : Deluxe
Difficulty of Transformation : Medium
Color Scheme : Black, "cheese" orangish yellow, clear plastic, and some semi-metallic gunmetal gray and light sky blue
Rating : 8.7

What's old is new again, amirite? To avoid the authorities, Bumblebee went and took on an old camaro form-- y'know, just like in the first movie . But this time, his colors are switched like they usually are only on his redecos, with black being the dominant color and orangish-yellow being only a secondary color-- surely the authorities will never suspect him now! Anyways, logic aside, this is a pretty darned awesome car mode. Its proportions are perfect-- as you'd expect from a toy with a licensed alt mode. There are some robot mode extras, but the only obvious ones are the bits of his waist and legs visible inside the clear windows (really, Hasbro, if you're going to make all the windows transparent, tint them dark enough that we can't so easily see the robot internals!). There's also just a touch of Bumblebee's heels visible from the back end, but this is getting picky. I've always liked the "reverse Bumblebee" color scheme, and this toy is no different; the yellow used sparsely looks better against a darker color than visa versa. The rear spoiler is painted, as well as a fairly nifty triple-stripe across his front above the grill (it should be around the whole front grill to be movie-accurate though, not just the top). His clear parts are painted partially black because of the mold breakup as well, and the black paint matches the black plastic perfectly, fortunately. (Also, clear rear windows for once! Yay!) That said, a few more orange-yellow paint apps to break up the black a bit more wouldn't have hurt-- the headlights and taillights are lacking paint, as well. The mold detailing is pretty sparse on this mode, with the wheels being the only parts with relatively intricate detailing, but it matches the source material, so I can't really complain there.
Hasbro's had plenty of previous tries to get Bumblebee's transformation at the deluxe size "right", and since this is Bumblebee before he gets his "upgrade" in the movie, his transformation and general look in robot mode (color scheme aside) is pretty familiar and sticks to a tried-and-true formula. The car doors and front wheels hang off his back as "wings"; his roof is partially collapsed behind his back; his chest is the car front broken up a bit; and the leg transformation is pretty much exactly the same as it's always been for his previous movie deluxe toys. There are a few slight differences, though; for one, the panels on top of the chest flip up. I have mixed feelings about this; on the one hand, it makes his chest look fragmented like in the movie, but on the other hand, it leaves a bit of a gap in between his head and the front of his chest, which is a little unsightly. His proportions are largely good, though his arms could stand to be a little longer and thicker (in fact, as a whole this toy is a tad smaller than previous movie Bumblebee deluxes, showing how Hasbro is dealing with cost-cutting). The roof sitting behind his back doesn't fold up as much as I'd like and sticks out some, but it doesn't get in the way of articulation so I don't mind it much. Same with the door/tire wings; I wish they connected to each other instead of making it look like 'Bee has four wings, but they still stay out of the way and keep Bumblebee's general silhouette the same. There aren't any little annoying pieces of vehicle kibble on his knees this time around, thankfully, with the rear end of his car mode instead hanging behind his legs near the ankles; I quite like this take, as those little bits of his Camaro mode on his hips/knees nearly always bugged me. The mold detailing on Bumblebee's robot parts is quite accurate to his movie model, with pistons, a fake gas cap, and the like molded onto the various parts of his body. His headsculpt is spot-on, though he can't flip down his battlemask on this version like he could on some other versions. The plastic color on his upper legs and arms is a really neat shade-- it's not paint, but it almost looks like it is, being a rather metallic-looking shade of gunmetal gray (see, Hasbro? THIS is how you do gray plastic). All this said, beyond the head this mode is pretty sparse in paint apps, the toes being the only other robot parts painted; a few more metallic gunmetal gray paint apps wouldn't have hurt, particularly around the waist. For articulation, Bumblebee can move at the neck, shoulders (at three points), elbows (at two points), hips (at two points), knees (at two points), and ankles (at two points)-- in other words, standard deluxe movie 'Bee articulation. As a gimmick, 'Bee's right arm has a push-missile launcher to give him some firepower.
"High Octane" Bumblebee is a pretty nice toy overall, with only a few minor kibble issues and lack of paint in a few areas holding the toy back itself. If you're a Bumblebee fan, but all means get this toy; that said, if you have a multiple deluxe movie Bumblebee toys already, this general design doesn't add anything new beyond having the same transformation applied to a retro Camaro; there aren't many surprises regarding this toy, for better or worse.


Bumblebee (High Octane) Bio:
To avoid detection, Bumblebee takes on a different form, disguising himself as a super-charged muscle car!
