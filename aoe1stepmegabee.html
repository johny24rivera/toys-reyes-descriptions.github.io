Bumblebee (Mega 1-Step) [AoE]

Allegiance: Autobot
Price: $30 (U.S.)
Difficulty of Transformation : Very Easy
Color Scheme : Milky orangish "cheese" yellow, black, metallic black, and some light metallic silvery blue and charcoal grayish black
Rating : 6.5

Two one-step changers just ain't enough for ol' Bumblebee-- no sir, he's got a BIG version of his Concept Camaro design in the AoE movie, as well! In vehicle mode, Bumblebee is quite large-- mass-wise, he's a bit bigger than a Leader class figure, though obviously much simpler both in his construction and in his detailing. As you'd expect from a licensed vehicle mode, his Camaro mode is pretty accurate when it comes to the proportions-- there really isn't any robot extras in this mode at all, not even on the back end. That said, a couple of oddities do exist because of the transformation, such as the rather ugly bumps in the front hood, odd lines along his roof, and little "chunks" taken out of the back window, which become the feet in robot mode. This last bit wouldn't be so bad if the tips of the pieces were painted the same nice silvery metallic blue as the other windows on 'Bee, but they aren't, which makes them look rather distracting. Overall Bumblebee's mold detailing is pretty much what it needs to be in this mode, and little more-- the grill and headlights are detailed nicely, along with the tires, but otherwise he's fairly sleek as you'd expect from a modern Camaro sportscar. The paint detailing is largely good-- you've got the windows painted a nice metallic silvery blue, the bordered stripe running down the center, and some black paint along the side and front rims, as well as filling in the grill to help break up all the orangish yellow that's very much your typical plastic color on a movie Bumblebee toy. All this said, at this size/price point not painting things like the taillights and rear bumper, wheel hubs, and the little "smaller" side windows is rather inexcusable.
Bumblebee transforms in the same way as the regular-sized 1-step "switchblade" Transformers, with you taking the sides and rotating them downwards, with everything else rotating into place for the robot mode as you do so. Because of the increased size/budget, though, a couple of extra things happen; for one, little feet details pop out at the bottom, and the rear roof sections slide to the sides of the legs to unveil more robot mold detailing. Other than those extra little bits, Bumblebee largely suffers/benefits from the same things that the other "switchblade"-formers do in robot mode; that is, from a side view he looks very odd and a bit too two-dimensional, with the chest just obviously fake detailing on a panel that hangs over his central mass and disguises (from the front, anyway) the fact that Bumblebee's legs go up almost to his neck in this mode. Best just to look at this mode from a frontal view. The legs are still a bit oversized, though, and the head undersized. The mold detailing is a bit more extensive in this mode, though not as impressive as I'd expect it to be on a movie toy, "simplified" or not-- the angular detailing on the legs in particular just looks too simplistic, as does the mold detailing on the backside (though to be fair, that detailing is facing backwards/downwards in both modes). The chest detailing is a bit better, though, with mostly accurate-to-the-movie details but with a boxier border. His head is sculpted quite well, though I wish his helmet-forehead thing could swing down over his face. His arms are also detailed a bit better, with some little intricate pistons here and there-- unfortunately, though, since his arms are mostly black, you can't see these more intricate details much. The metallic black paint apps are spread VERY well across the upper legs, head, and chest, contrasting mighty well against all that orangish yellow and definitely drawing the eye. The metallic light blue eyes look good, though 'Bee's face could stand to be a shade or two lighter. Just as with the mold detailing, though, the paint detailing on the lower legs is strangely lacking (basically nonexistent on the robot-specific parts), another disappointment down there. Bumblebee's articulation is slightly better than other "switchblade"-formers, but only slightly-- he can move at the shoulders (at two points) and elbows (at two points), but no movement anywhere else-- c'mon Hasbro, not even a head twist? Bumblebee does have one deployable gimmick, and that's a starred "ninja wheel" projectile that can be launched from the right lower arm by pulling back on a grayish black tab on the other side of said arm. It's solely push-activated, but travels farther than you'd think.
The "Mega" 1-Step version of AoE Bumblebee I'd only recommend to a very specific audience. Yes, the 1-step line is aimed at a younger audience, but even more specific than that; given that he's really not much better than a regular switchblade-former, but is 3x the price, this is only recommended for both A. An individual who wants a simple toy of "modern" AoE Bumblebee but doesn't have the manual dexterity for the smaller 1-stepers, and B. Is willing to plunk down (or have their parents plunk down for them) $30 for a simple toy with admittedly little playability/gimmicks for such a price. That's a REALLY tiny audience, and honestly this is one of those movie toys that just probably shouldn't have been made.

Review by Beastbot
